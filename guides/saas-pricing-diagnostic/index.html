Why a generic pricing model doesn't tell you your price

Search "saas pricing models" and you get the same five every time: flat-rate, tiered, per-seat, usage-based, freemium. All real. None of them tell you what your product should charge.

The model is the easy part. The hard part is the value metric — the unit you charge against — and the number attached to it. That depends on your product, who pays, your stage, and what you're afraid of. A list can't see those. A diagnosis can.

Marcus on pricing
"I recommend you pick your value metric before your price. The metric is what scales revenue when a customer gets more value. Get that wrong and no number saves you."

What the diagnostic actually does

It asks six things: what your product does, who pays, your core value unit, your stage, your current price, and the one outcome you're most afraid of. Then it returns a single recommendation — model, starting price, packaging, and the first fix — instead of a menu you still have to choose from.

One recommendation is the point. Most pricing advice hands you options and calls it help. A menu is not a decision. If you want the reasoning behind the call, read the full guide to pricing a SaaS product, which walks through each model and when it fits.

Built for solo founders, not pricing committees

If you're a solopreneur or an indie hacker, you don't have a pricing team, a finance hire, or six months of cohort data. You have a product and a decision to make this week. The diagnostic is shaped for that — see the solopreneur SaaS pricing strategy for the longer playbook.

Two follow-on decisions come up the moment you have a number: how to package it, and whether to bill monthly or annually. The diagnostic flags the packaging shape, and the annual vs monthly breakdown covers the billing call.
