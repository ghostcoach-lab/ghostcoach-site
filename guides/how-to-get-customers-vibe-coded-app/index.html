How to get customers for your vibe-coded app

Product Hunt gave you 400 upvotes and 2 paying customers. Here is why — and what to do instead to build an actual acquisition system.

This is the most common situation in vibe coding: you shipped a real product, posted on Product Hunt, got a healthy number of upvotes, hundreds of signups — and ended up with 2 or 3 paying customers. The product works. The problem is not the product. The problem is that Product Hunt is not an acquisition system.

Why Product Hunt is not an acquisition system

Product Hunt generates one thing: a spike of attention from people who are interested in new products. That audience is different from your actual customers. The overlap — people who browse Product Hunt who also have the specific problem your product solves — is much smaller than the upvote count suggests.

A Product Hunt launch is a launch event. It generates a burst of traffic for 24–72 hours and then stops entirely. An acquisition system is different: it is a repeatable process that brings in customers every week, independently of what you launch. Most vibe-coded products have one but not the other.

How to find your first 10 paying customers

The first 10 paying customers for any vibe-coded product almost always come from the same place: direct, personal outreach to people who have the specific problem your product solves. Not cold email blasts. Personal messages to specific people.

The process:

  1. Find where your customer talks about the problem. Reddit threads, Twitter/X discussions, Indie Hackers posts, specific Slack communities, LinkedIn groups. Open a browser and find 50 people who have said something like "I wish there was a tool that..." or "does anyone know how to..." in relation to the problem your product solves.
  2. Send a message that shows you saw their specific comment. Not a pitch. "I noticed you mentioned [specific problem] last week. I built something that fixes exactly that — here is a link if you want to try it free." One sentence of context, one sentence of offer.
  3. Do 50 of these before you touch another acquisition channel. This is the fastest path to your first 10 customers. It does not scale, which is why most people avoid it. But it gets you signal — who responds, what they say, whether they pay — that no amount of ads or content can replicate.

The direct outreach method that converts

The message that works has three components: it references something specific they said or did, it names the problem precisely, and it makes a zero-friction offer. Here is the template:

"I saw your post in [community] about [specific problem]. I built something that handles exactly this — [one-sentence description]. Happy to give you free access for a month if you want to try it. [Link]"

What makes this work: it is specific enough to prove you are not spamming, it names their problem (not your solution), and it removes the barrier of payment. The goal of the first message is not a sale — it is a conversation.

For conversion: follow up once after 5 days if you hear nothing. One follow-up, specific, short. After that, move on. Pestering is not a customer acquisition strategy.

Building a content channel for vibe coders

Once you have your first 10 customers, content is the highest-leverage channel for sustained acquisition. The key distinction: content about the problem converts. Content about the product does not.

"Why invoicing for creative freelancers is broken" gets shared by people with that problem. "Our new invoice template feature" gets shared by nobody. Write about the problem your product solves, not about the product.

The platform depends on where your customer is:

  • Indie Hackers / r/SaaS: If your customer is a solo builder, these communities are high-quality and trust thoughtful posts
  • Twitter/X: Short, specific insight posts — "here is a mistake I see founders make about X" — build an audience that converts
  • SEO: Slower, but compounds. Target the exact phrases your customer types into Google when they have the problem

From first 10 to repeatable growth

The transition from 10 customers to 100 customers requires moving from personal outreach to a channel that scales. This is not a simultaneous switch — it is a sequential one. Get to 10 customers through outreach, then use what you learn (who they are, where they came from, what they said) to design the scalable channel.

The question to answer: can you trace your existing 10 customers back to a specific source? A particular community, a type of content, a specific platform? That source is your channel. Build it systematically for 90 days before trying anything else.
